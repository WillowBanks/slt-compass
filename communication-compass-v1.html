How SLTs Work Within the NZ Ministry of Education

What They Do

Ministry of Education SLTs support children and young people (typically ages 3–21) with communication and swallowing needs in educational settings. They work in schools, early childhood centres, and specialist units — not private clinics.

How Referrals Work

Referrals come through schools, parents, or other professionals. The SLT reviews the referral, may observe the child in their learning environment, and decides on next steps. Wait times vary by region and demand.

Assessment Process

Assessment uses a mix of standardised tests, observations, and conversations with teachers and whanau. The goal is to understand how the child communicates in real life — not just in a test room.

Planning Support

After assessment, the SLT creates a plan with specific goals. This is shared with teachers and family so everyone is working towards the same outcomes. Goals are reviewed regularly.

Tiered Service Delivery

Universal: Whole-class strategies any teacher can use.
Targeted: Small-group support for children who need more help.
Specialist: Individual, intensive support for complex needs.

Working With Families

Families are key partners. SLTs share strategies for home, explain what's happening and why, and adjust plans based on family feedback. You know your child best — the SLT brings clinical expertise to combine with your knowledge.

Questions to Ask Your SLT
  • Q What specific area of communication are you most concerned about for my child?
  • Q What does "age-appropriate" look like for my child's age — what should I realistically expect?
  • Q What can I do at home to support what you're working on?
  • Q How will we know if the approach is working — what are the signs of progress?
  • Q How long does this type of support usually take before we see changes?
  • Q Are there any apps, books, or resources you'd recommend for our situation?
  • Q Should I correct my child when they make errors, or is there a better way to respond?
  • Q Will my child's difficulties affect their reading and writing as they get older?
Home Practice Ideas (adapted for 5–8 year olds)
  • 1 Narrate your day: Talk through what you're doing — cooking, driving, tidying. This gives your child a rich language model without any pressure to respond.
  • 2 Read together daily: Even 10 minutes of shared reading builds vocabulary, comprehension, and narrative skills. Pause and ask "what do you think happens next?"
  • 3 Play word games: "I spy" for sounds (not letters), rhyming games, or "tell me a word that starts with..." — these build phonological awareness naturally.
  • 4 Expand, don't correct: If your child says "him goed park," respond with "Yes! He went to the park" — modelling the correct form without making them feel wrong.
  • 5 Give processing time: After asking a question, wait 5–10 seconds. Many children need longer to formulate their response than adults expect.
  • 6 Limit screen time, maximise talk time: Real conversations with real people are irreplaceable for language development. Screens are passive; talking is active.
